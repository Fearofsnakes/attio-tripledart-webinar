# Attio: competitive set

> The alternatives Attio implicitly or explicitly positions against, from their site, June 2026. The positioning-audit skill uses this set to score differentiation and find white space.

| Competitor | How Attio frames them | Where Attio claims an edge | Where the competitor is strong |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Salesforce** | Enterprise, legacy architecture | Modern, AI-native, faster to set up, flexible data model | Enterprise depth, ecosystem, trust at scale |
| **HubSpot** | Mid-market, AI added on top | AI built in from the start, not retrofitted | Brand, all-in-one suite, marketing + CRM bundle |
| **Zoho** | Budget option, shallow AI | Deeper, structural AI | Price, breadth |
| **Notion / Airtable** | Flexible but not a real CRM | CRM capabilities plus flexibility | Flexibility, low cost, familiarity |

## Adjacent / newer alternatives to consider (verify relevance)

- **Folk**: relationship-first CRM for small teams and agencies
- **Pipedrive**: sales-pipeline-first SMB CRM
- **Clay**: not a CRM, but competes for the "AI does GTM work" mindshare and budget

## White-space prompts for the audit

- Everyone now says "AI-native." What does Attio own that a competitor cannot simply also claim?
- "Flexible data model" is a feature, not a position. What is the buyer outcome that flexibility unlocks that rivals cannot match?
- "For startups and builders" is an audience, not a wedge. Is there a sharper wedge inside that audience (a specific motion, a specific moment) where Attio is the obvious pick?

> These prompts are starting points. The live `positioning-audit` run fills the real scorecard and the real white space.
